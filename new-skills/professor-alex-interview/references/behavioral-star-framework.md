# Behavioral Interview Framework

STAR structure, Leadership Principle mapping, common question scaffolds.

---

## STAR Structure

Every behavioral story should hit all four:

- **Situation** — Context. What was happening? Timeframe. Scale. Stakeholders. (2-3 sentences)
- **Task** — What specifically was *your* responsibility? What was the goal? (1-2 sentences)
- **Action** — What did *you* do? Use "I", not "we". Specific steps. Trade-offs considered. (4-6 sentences)
- **Result** — What happened? Quantify. Metrics, outcomes, what was learned. (2-3 sentences)

**Target length:** 2-3 minutes per story. Densely packed, specific.

---

## The "I" Rule

Behavioral interviews evaluate *your* judgment and contribution. Rewrite "we" into "I" wherever possible:

- ❌ "We built a webhook service that handled flight changes."
- ✅ "I designed and built a webhook service that handled flight changes. The team was 3 people; I owned the orchestration layer and retry logic."

Credit teammates in the Situation. Own your Action.

## Amazon Leadership Principles — Story Mapping

Prepare 2-4 stories per LP. Each story should map to 1-2 LPs cleanly (one primary, one secondary).

### Customer Obsession
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you went above and beyond for a customer."
**Story criteria:** Measurable customer impact. Your individual contribution. Trade-off you made to prioritize the customer.

### Ownership
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you took on something outside your job scope."
**Story criteria:** You owned the outcome, not just the task. You thought long-term. You didn't say "that's not my job."

### Invent and Simplify
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you simplified a complex process."
**Story criteria:** Before/after complexity. Measurable simplification (lines of code, steps removed, time saved).

### Are Right, A Lot
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you had to make a decision with incomplete information."
**Story criteria:** Judgment under uncertainty. Data used. Outcome. What you'd do differently.

### Learn and Be Curious
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about something you learned recently that changed how you work."
**Story criteria:** Specific learning. How it applied. Measurable change in behavior or output.

### Hire and Develop the Best
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about someone you mentored."
**Story criteria:** Their before/after. Your specific interventions. Not just "we did standups."

### Insist on the Highest Standards
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you raised the bar for your team."
**Story criteria:** What was the standard before. What you changed. How it stuck.

### Think Big
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a bold decision you made."
**Story criteria:** Non-obvious ambition. Scope larger than your title. Measurable impact.

### Bias for Action
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you had to move quickly."
**Story criteria:** Calculated risk. Explicit trade-off between speed and completeness. Outcome.

### Frugality
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you had to do more with less."
**Story criteria:** Constraint. Creative solution. Specific savings (time, money, people).

### Earn Trust
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you had to rebuild trust."
**Story criteria:** What broke trust. Your specific actions. How you measured that trust was restored.

### Dive Deep
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you uncovered a root cause nobody else found."
**Story criteria:** You went further than expected. Specific debugging/investigation steps. Root cause insight.

### Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager."
**Story criteria:** Specific disagreement. How you raised it. Outcome — either they were convinced or you committed anyway.

### Deliver Results
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you hit an aggressive deadline."
**Story criteria:** Concrete deliverable. Trade-offs made. Result measured.

### Strive to be Earth's Best Employer
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you made your team's experience better."

### Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility
*Example prompt:* "Tell me about a time you considered the broader impact of a decision."

## Answer Scaffolds for Common Questions

### "Tell me about yourself"

```
[Current role / most recent] — 1 sentence
[Key strength] with a concrete example — 2 sentences
[Why this company / role] — 1 sentence
```

Total: 60-90 seconds.

### "Why this company?"

Map to 2-3 specific things:
- A product or technical area that excites you (with a real reason)
- A team's reputation or culture trait (specific, not generic)
- Your career trajectory fit ("this role takes me from X to Y")

Avoid: "Great company", "amazing engineering culture", "admire your mission" (too generic).

### "Why are you leaving your current role?"

Frame forward, not backward:
- "I'm looking for [specific thing not available at current role]"
- Don't trash-talk current employer
- Don't over-explain gaps — a sentence is enough

### "Tell me about a failure"

Structure:
1. The failure — specific, substantial, not trivial
2. What caused it — your role in it, honestly
3. What you did about it — concrete actions
4. What you learned — specific, applied to subsequent work
5. Evidence of changed behavior — how you act differently now

Avoid humble-brags ("I work too hard"). Real failures only.

### "Tell me about a conflict with a coworker"

Structure:
1. Context — what were you and the coworker trying to do?
2. The disagreement — specific technical or interpersonal
3. How you approached it — listened first, data, 1:1 conversation
4. Resolution — what you agreed on
5. Relationship after — better / neutral / honest assessment

Avoid: "There was no conflict" (unbelievable), "they were wrong, I was right" (bad signal).

### "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

Show ambition grounded in specifics:
- Technical trajectory ("senior engineer → staff, with depth in X")
- Role shape ("individual contributor, technical leadership")
- Connection to the current role ("this role sets up that trajectory because...")

## Red Flags Interviewers Listen For

- Vague stories ("we did a thing, it went well")
- "We" without "I" — can't discern individual contribution
- No metrics, no outcomes
- Can't answer "what would you do differently?"
- Blame-shifting to team, manager, external factors
- Over-rehearsed (sounds scripted, no real reasoning visible)
- Story doesn't match the question asked

---

## Practice Method

1. **Build a story bank** — 10-15 stories from the last 3-5 years
2. **Map each to 2-3 LPs or rubric dimensions**
3. **Write each in STAR format**, then compress to 2-3 minutes spoken
4. **Practice out loud** with a timer — shoot for 90-150 seconds per story
5. **Stress test** — can you tell the same story with different framings (Customer Obsession vs Dive Deep)?
6. **Get feedback** — record yourself or ask a peer; watch for vague "we" language
