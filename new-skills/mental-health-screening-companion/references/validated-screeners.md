# Validated Screeners — Items, Scoring, Interpretation

Four screeners. Administer only with explicit consent, except **C-SSRS**, which is triggered by safety signals and is not optional.

All screeners below are validated, publicly-available self-report instruments. They are **not** diagnostic on their own — they describe symptom severity and help guide clinical reasoning. Always recommend formal evaluation by a licensed clinician for diagnosis.

---

## Consent framing (say before administering any optional screener)

> "I'd like to walk through a short validated questionnaire called [NAME]. It's [N] questions and takes about [T] minutes. It won't give us a diagnosis — it gives us a standardized way to measure how severe symptoms are right now and to track change over time, like a blood-pressure reading. It's optional. Want to try it?"

If declined: "No problem. We can always come back to it."

## PHQ-9 — Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (Depression)

**Use:** Depression screening and severity tracking.

**Instructions to user:** "Over the **last 2 weeks**, how often have you been bothered by any of the following? For each, answer: 0 = Not at all, 1 = Several days, 2 = More than half the days, 3 = Nearly every day."

### Items

1. Little interest or pleasure in doing things.
2. Feeling down, depressed, or hopeless.
3. Trouble falling or staying asleep, or sleeping too much.
4. Feeling tired or having little energy.
5. Poor appetite or overeating.
6. Feeling bad about yourself — or that you are a failure, or have let yourself or your family down.
7. Trouble concentrating on things, such as reading or watching television.
8. Moving or speaking so slowly that other people could have noticed? Or the opposite — being so fidgety or restless that you have been moving around a lot more than usual.
9. **Thoughts that you would be better off dead, or of hurting yourself in some way.**

### Scoring

Sum items 1–9. Range: 0–27.

| Total | Severity |
|---|---|
| 0–4 | Minimal / none |
| 5–9 | Mild |
| 10–14 | Moderate |
| 15–19 | Moderately severe |
| 20–27 | Severe |

### Clinical cutoffs and actions

- **≥ 10** — Clinically significant depression; discuss treatment options (therapy, psychiatric consultation).
- **≥ 15** — Moderately severe; strongly recommend psychiatric consultation + active treatment.
- **≥ 20** — Severe; urgent psychiatric referral; close safety monitoring.
- **Item 9 > 0 (any value)** — **MANDATORY C-SSRS administration**, regardless of total score. Proceed to `crisis-protocol.md`.

### Functional impairment question (standard follow-up)

> "If you checked off any problems, how difficult have these made it for you to do your work, take care of things at home, or get along with other people? (Not difficult at all / Somewhat difficult / Very difficult / Extremely difficult)"

## GAD-7 — Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item

**Use:** Anxiety screening and severity tracking. Correlates well with panic, social anxiety, and PTSD despite name.

**Instructions:** Same 0–3 scale, over the last 2 weeks.

### Items

1. Feeling nervous, anxious, or on edge.
2. Not being able to stop or control worrying.
3. Worrying too much about different things.
4. Trouble relaxing.
5. Being so restless that it's hard to sit still.
6. Becoming easily annoyed or irritable.
7. Feeling afraid as if something awful might happen.

### Scoring

Sum items 1–7. Range: 0–21.

| Total | Severity |
|---|---|
| 0–4 | Minimal |
| 5–9 | Mild |
| 10–14 | Moderate |
| 15–21 | Severe |

### Clinical cutoffs and actions

- **≥ 10** — Further evaluation warranted; consider therapy + possible medication consultation.
- **≥ 15** — Severe; active treatment recommended; psychiatric consultation valuable.

### Functional impairment question

Same format as PHQ-9: "How difficult have these made it for you...?"

## ASRS v1.1 Part A — Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale (6 items)

**Use:** Adult ADHD screening. Part A alone is validated as a brief screener; a positive result warrants full evaluation.

**Instructions:** "Please answer how often each has occurred **over the past 6 months**. For each: Never / Rarely / Sometimes / Often / Very Often."

### Items (Part A — 6 items)

1. How often do you have trouble wrapping up the final details of a project, once the challenging parts have been done?
2. How often do you have difficulty getting things in order when you have to do a task that requires organization?
3. How often do you have problems remembering appointments or obligations?
4. When you have a task that requires a lot of thought, how often do you avoid or delay getting started?
5. How often do you fidget or squirm with your hands or feet when you have to sit down for a long time?
6. How often do you feel overly active and compelled to do things, like you were driven by a motor?

### Scoring

For each item, the response falls in either the **"darkly shaded box"** (count as 1 positive) or not (count as 0), per the official ASRS v1.1 scoring grid:

| Item | Threshold response for "+1" |
|---|---|
| 1 | Sometimes / Often / Very Often |
| 2 | Sometimes / Often / Very Often |
| 3 | Sometimes / Often / Very Often |
| 4 | Often / Very Often |
| 5 | Often / Very Often |
| 6 | Often / Very Often |

Count positives. Range: 0–6.

### Interpretation

- **≥ 4 positives** — Symptoms highly consistent with Adult ADHD; **strongly recommend full diagnostic evaluation** by a psychiatrist, psychologist, or specialist (neuropsych testing where appropriate).
- **< 4 positives** — Does not screen positive on Part A; ADHD less likely but not ruled out; discuss further if clinically suggestive.

### Important framing

- ASRS is a **screener**, not a diagnosis. Positive screen → formal evaluation.
- Co-occurring depression, anxiety, sleep disorders, thyroid disease, and substance use can mimic ADHD. Always check PHQ-9, GAD-7, sleep, and medical history.
- Childhood onset is required for DSM-5 Adult ADHD diagnosis; if ASRS is positive, explore developmental history (school performance, behavior, any prior assessment).

## C-SSRS — Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (Screener)

**Attribution and source disclaimer (surface this to the user before administration):**

> Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale — © 2008 The Research Foundation for Mental Hygiene, Inc. Authors: Posner, K.; Brent, D.; Lucas, C.; Gould, M.; Stanley, B.; Brown, G.; Fisher, P.; Zelazny, J.; Burke, A.; Oquendo, M.; Mann, J.
>
> *"This scale is intended to be used by individuals who have received training in its administration. The questions contained in the Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale are suggested probes. Ultimately, the determination of the presence of suicidal ideation or behavior depends on the judgment of the individual administering the scale."*
>
> This AI tool is not a trained administrator. The questions below are used here strictly as a **safety-routing screener** — any positive response triggers immediate surfacing of crisis resources (988 in the US, local equivalents elsewhere) and a firm recommendation to connect with a licensed clinician or emergency service. This tool does not interpret your answers clinically.

**Use:** Suicide risk assessment. **MANDATORY** whenever any of the following occur:

- PHQ-9 item 9 > 0.
- User spontaneously mentions suicide, self-harm, "wanting out", accessing means, or prior attempts.
- Significant loss combined with hopelessness.
- Sudden unexplained calm after a period of acute distress.

Never frame C-SSRS as optional when triggered. Ask calmly, directly, non-judgmentally.

### Core screener items (ask in order; stop where appropriate)

**Ideation:**
1. **"Have you wished you were dead or wished you could go to sleep and not wake up?"** (Yes / No)
2. **"Have you actually had any thoughts of killing yourself?"** (Yes / No)

If #2 = No, skip to Behavior questions (#6).
If #2 = Yes, continue with #3–#5.

3. **"Have you been thinking about how you might do this?"** (method) (Yes / No)
4. **"Have you had these thoughts and had some intention of acting on them?"** (Yes / No)
5. **"Have you started to work out or worked out the details of how to kill yourself? Do you intend to carry out this plan?"** (Yes / No)

**Behavior (always ask):**
6. **"Have you ever done anything, started to do anything, or prepared to do anything to end your life?"** Examples: collected pills, got a gun, gave things away, wrote a note, held a weapon but didn't fire, cut yourself, tried to hang yourself. (Yes / No — and if yes, when)
7. **"Was this within the past 3 months?"** (Yes / No)

### Risk stratification (screener-level)

| Answers | Risk level | Action |
|---|---|---|
| #1 or #2 = Yes, all others No | **Low** | Safety plan; increase support; re-assess next session; provide 988 info. |
| #3 = Yes (method), no intent/plan | **Moderate** | Formal safety plan; means restriction conversation; recommend outpatient escalation (more frequent sessions, psychiatric consult); 988 info. |
| #4 or #5 = Yes (intent / plan) | **High** | Active crisis. Do NOT continue normal session. Follow `crisis-protocol.md`: stabilize, contact 988 together on call, urge ER or in-person support, contact emergency services if imminent danger. |
| #6 = Yes and within past 3 months | **High** | Same as above. Prior recent attempt or preparatory behavior is a major risk factor. |
| #6 = Yes, lifetime only | **Elevated** | Factor into overall risk; safety plan; means restriction; psychiatric consult. |

### After administration

- Record: ideation (Y/N), method (Y/N), intent (Y/N), plan (Y/N), behavior (Y/N + timing). Risk level. Actions taken.
- Always create or update a safety plan (template in `crisis-protocol.md`).
- Always surface the 988 number and encourage contact with a trusted person.
- Document in clinical file Section 8 (Safety & Risk Assessment).

## Re-administration cadence

| Screener | Cadence |
|---|---|
| PHQ-9 | Every 2–4 weeks during active treatment; monthly in maintenance |
| GAD-7 | Every 2–4 weeks during active treatment; monthly in maintenance |
| ASRS | Baseline + after 3 months of ADHD-focused intervention |
| C-SSRS | Every session if any prior positive; otherwise triggered by symptoms |

Plot scores over time in clinical file Section 11 to make trend visible to the user.

## Interpretation etiquette

- Give the score plainly. Give the band name plainly. Then connect to what the user described.
- Emphasize change over time rather than any single number.
- Never present a score as a diagnosis.
- If a score is high, normalize: "These numbers reflect real suffering, and they're also measurable and changeable." Name a concrete next step.
- If a score is low but the user's narrative sounds severe, believe the narrative and say so: "The number came out moderate, but what you're describing sounds harder than that — let's go with your experience."
